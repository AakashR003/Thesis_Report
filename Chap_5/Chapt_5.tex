\chapter{Multi-Frequency Simulations}
\label{cha:multi_frequency}

This chapter presents a series of multi-frequency forced-motion CFD simulations performed to answer the questions 
formulated in Chapter 1: whether the number of superposed frequencies influences the accuracy of flutter derivative 
identification, how many superpositions are appropriate, which parameter limits this number, and where any methodological 
error originates.
Simulation parameters are first defined on the basis of the signal analysis and single-frequency simulations presented 
earlier.
These parameters are then applied in two distinct multi-frequency campaigns in which the frequency superpositions 
are kept the same, but the prescribed motion amplitudes are varied.
Finally, based on the results and inferences obtained from both campaigns, a practical guideline for the selection of frequencies and amplitudes in multi-frequency simulations is proposed.
The chapter proceeds as follows. Section 1 defines the simulation parameters, including the selection of frequencies and 
the superposition schemes.
Section 2 presents the first multi-frequency campaign together with the detailed procedure of 
the multi-frequency method using the fast Fourier transform.
Section 3 describes the second multi-frequency campaign. 
Section 4 formulates the recommended guideline for conducting multi-frequency simulations.

% ============================================================
\section{Simulation Parameters}
% ============================================================

The following criteria govern the selection of frequencies and amplitudes for the 
multi-frequency simulations:

\begin{enumerate}
    \setlength{\itemsep}{0pt}\setlength{\parskip}{0pt}
    \item Frequencies must lie on the frequency bins, as derived from the signal analysis.
    \item Prescribed motion amplitudes must be less than the threshold amplitude for linearity limit, as derived from the single frequency simulation tests.
\end{enumerate}

All other simulation parameters are kept the same.\\

Based on these criteria, two sets of multi-frequency campaigns are performed.
For both simulation campaigns, the frequency superpositions are kept identical, but the prescribed motion amplitude is varied.
In the first campaign, the sum of the heave and pitch motion amplitudes for each 
superposition is kept below the linearity threshold.
In the second campaign, the individual heave and pitch amplitudes are kept below the 
threshold.
Since the frequency selection is the same for both campaigns, the frequency superpositions are described once in this section, while the amplitude selections are described in their corresponding analysis sections.\\

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Selection of Frequencies and Superpositions}
\label{sec:Selection of Frequencies and Superpositions}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

To evaluate whether increasing the number of superimposed frequencies affects 
the accuracy of flutter derivative identification, eight frequencies are selected and 
organized into four superposition schemes.
Superposition Scheme~1 consists of single-frequency 
simulations and serves as the reference case.
In Superposition Schemes~2, 3, and~4, these 
frequencies are grouped into subsets of two, three, and four superpositions, respectively.
The frequency selection procedure and the resulting superpositions 
are described below.\\

Within each superposition scheme, 
the lowest frequency (corresponding to the highest reduced velocity) is 
chosen first, with all other frequencies in the scheme selected relative to it.
The lowest frequency may be chosen independently, but one important condition must be satisfied. Its frequency bin spacing 
must be a definite number, so that the dependent frequencies fall exactly on the frequency bins.
To illustrate, if the lowest frequency is set to $0.133\dots$~Hz, the resulting frequency bins would be $0.133\dots$~Hz, $0.266\dots$~Hz, $0.40$~Hz, 
and so forth (determined by multiplying by integer values).
When the integer is a multiple of 3, the bins yield exact values; 
otherwise, they produce inexact numbers.
For instance, if a dependent frequency within the set is set to $0.266$~Hz, 
the mismatch between the prescribed frequency ($0.266$) and the bin frequency ($0.26666\dots$) will prevent proper alignment with the bin, 
resulting in spectral leakage that causes errors in the results.
To avoid this issue, it is essential to select the lowest 
frequency as a precise, terminating decimal number, thereby maximizing the range of usable frequency values within the bins.\\

For both campaigns in this investigation, the lowest frequency is set to $0.125$~Hz (corresponding reduced wind speed of $21.86$).\\

Allowing for 10 oscillations, the total simulation time is:
\begin{equation}
    t_{\text{total}} = \frac{1}{0.125} \times 10 = 80 \text{ s}
\end{equation}

With a time step size of $\Delta t = 0.002$~s, the total number of time steps is $40{,}000$.
The frequency bin spacing for a simulation with a stable time of 80 seconds is:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta f = \frac{1/\Delta t}{N} = \frac{500}{40000} = 0.0125 \text{ Hz}
\end{equation}

Hence, the frequency bins are $[0.0125,\ 0.0250,\ 0.0375,\ 0.0500,\ \ldots]$.\\

The frequency spacing and frequency bins depend on the number of time steps.
Therefore, if the simulation is interrupted in the middle, the aerodynamic force and moment cannot be extracted accurately, as the frequency bins and 
spacing will be altered.\\

Dependent frequencies in the scheme are selected from the frequency bin list.
An additional recommendation is to have considerable spacing between the frequencies in a superposition scheme
% avoid selecting 
% two frequencies that are adjacent within the frequency bin list.
If not followed, the result at the 
neighbouring frequency may also be corrupted through spectral leakage at the intermediate time steps.
To minimise this risk, sufficient spacing between selected frequencies is suggested.\\

The frequencies selected for simulation are shown in Table~\ref{tab:frequencies}.
Frequencies are selected such that each frequency can serve as the primary frequency, 
providing flexibility in forming superposition schemes.
The frequencies $0.15$~Hz and $0.25$~Hz are exceptions, 
as they are also needed to cover the reduced velocity range for accurate interpolation of the 
flutter derivative curve.\\

\subsection{Comparison of Flutter Derivatives}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{Figures/MultiFrequency_Study_1/Combined_Flutter_Derivative.png}
    \caption{Flutter derivatives identified from Multi-Frequency Campaign I across all superposition schemes}
    \label{fig:Combined_Flutter_Derivatives_1}
\end{figure}

A total of 34 CFD simulations are conducted, and the results are discussed in the current and following sections.
From the flutter derivative plot (Figure~\ref{fig:Combined_Flutter_Derivatives_1}), 
it can be observed that $H_1^*$, $A_1^*$, $H_3^*$, $A_3^*$, $H_4^*$, and $A_4^*$ 
agree well across all superposition schemes.
For Superposition Scheme~4, a slight error is 
introduced, which is located in the lower reduced velocity range (i.e., below $U_r = 6.83$).
Due to interpolation, this error propagates to higher reduced velocities, which explains the discrepancies 
occurring there.\\

It is also noted that $H_3^*$ and $A_3^*$ exhibit slightly varying values at the higher reduced velocities.
This can be inferred from the single frequency simulations, which indicates that $H_3^*$ and $A_3^*$ display mild 
nonlinearity at higher reduced velocities.
It is further noted that $H_4^*$ and $A_4^*$ show slightly varying values, although the magnitude of 
the error is not clearly visible in this figure.\\

For the flutter derivatives $H_2^*$ and $A_2^*$, the errors at lower reduced velocities are negligible, 
even for Superposition Scheme~4.
However, at higher reduced velocities, the error becomes significantly larger.
Based on the single frequency simulations, this is expected, as it reflects the nonlinearity due to the phase of the moment of 
the flutter derivative coefficients $H_2^*$ and $A_2^*$, and is not a consequence of the multi-frequency method itself.\\

The plot indicates that when the multi-frequency method is implemented in accordance with the principles 
underlying the Fourier transformation, the resulting errors are negligible.\\

\subsection{Error Assessment Against Single-Frequency Simulations}

In this section, the errors are quantified.
Since the source of error lies in the aerodynamic forces, 
moments, and phases (structural properties are assumed to be known), the error analysis is performed 
on the amplitude and phase of the forces with respect to the imposed motion.\\

Figure~\ref{fig: Amplitude_Errors} shows the lift force and moment magnitude error as a function of reduced velocity for each superposition scheme.
For heave motion (solid lines), the errors at higher reduced velocities are negligible.
Below the reduced velocity of 6.83, the error rises to up to 40\% for Superposition Scheme~4.
The reason for this behavior is investigated further in Section~\ref{sec:MF-I_FreqDomain}.
For pitch motion, slight errors are present at higher reduced velocities, which is consistent with the single frequency simulations
 and in agreement with the flutter derivative plot discussed in the previous section.\\

An important inference is that, although the total prescribed motion amplitude is the same across all four 
multi-frequency schemes, the results reflect the nonlinearity due to phase of pitch of the cross-section.
This shows the key distinction: not only must the peak amplitude be kept 
below the linearity threshold, but the instantaneous amplitude at every point in time must 
also remain within this limit.
This condition is satisfied only in the single-frequency case. 
Consequently, the multi-frequency method for $H_2^*$ and $A_2^*$ derivatives is unlikely yield results fully equivalent to single-frequency 
simulation results when the prescribed motion amplitude, chosen for pitch is below the limit of phase variations.\\

Conversely, flutter derivative identifications aim 
to simulate real-world conditions, where the induced motion is not a pure single frequency.
From this perspective, it can be argued that the multi-frequency method provides a more 
representative result than single-frequency oscillation (because of the inclusion of phase change).
Therefore, whether the deviations in $A_2^*$ and $H_2^*$ are acceptable depends on the perspective adopted 
and the intended use of the results.\\

\subsection{Spectral Analysis}
\label{sec:MF-I_FreqDomain}

In this section, the frequency domain of the aerodynamic forces is examined.
This study helps to further classify the source of nonlinearity, specifically, whether it arises from higher-order 
terms induced by large heave and pitch amplitudes, or from the nonlinearity due to the phase of pitch motion of the 
cross-section.\\

From Figures~\ref{fig:Frequency_Domain_Analysis_Heave_MF-I} and~\ref{fig:Frequency_Domain_Analysis_Pitch_MF-I},
the frequency spectra for the 4th superposition schemes are without spurious frequencies, showing only the static 
component, the forced-motion response components, and the vortex shedding component.
This is observed for the other 2 superposition schemes as well.\\
This strengthens the argument from previous sections: provided the total amplitude remains below the 
linearity limit, no higher-order harmonic terms appear in the aerodynamic response.\\

It should also be noted that if the multi-frequency method is adopted for flutter derivative 
identification, the only outputs available for inspection are the flutter derivative plot and 
the spectral analysis.
The nonlinearity due to the phase of pitch motion of the cross-section therefore cannot 
be detected from these outputs alone.\\

This reasoning is further supported by an additional observation. In Superposition Scheme~3, the aerodynamic forces for the reduced velocity 
of 3.42 can be calculated from two simulation groups (Table~\ref{tab:MF-I_SP3}, MF-I-14 and MF-I-15).
Following the same reasoning, the error for MF-I-15 of Scheme~3 (which pairs 3.42 with lower reduced velocities) should be greater than that 
for MF-I-14 (which pairs 3.42 with higher reduced velocities).
This is indeed the case. For the $H_1^*$ derivative, the error for MF-I-14 of Superposition Scheme~3 is 0.156\%, while 
for MF-I-15 the error is 11.913\%.\\

This effect is less significant for pitch motion because the ratio of 
aerodynamic lift force between the two extreme reduced velocities within a superposition scheme group is  
smaller in pitch than in heave.
Numerically, this ratio is 28.32 for the heave lift force and 3.186 for the pitch lift force.
This is the reason that the effect does not cause significant error in the pitch case. 

This effect can be reduced by ensuring that the amplitudes of the aerodynamic forces at the different frequencies in 
the superposition are of comparable magnitude. Since the resulting aerodynamic force is dependent on the prescribed motion 
amplitude, the amplitude parameters can be adjusted accordingly by using a smaller amplitude at lower reduced velocities 
and a larger amplitude at higher reduced velocities. However, the exact ratio between these amplitudes is not known in advance, 
as the aerodynamic forces are known only after the CFD simulations. Therefore, a similar ratio from the present study 
can be adopted as a reference, but it should be used with caution and carefully verified after performing the CFD simulations 
to ensure the ratio does not become excessively large. \\

\section{Discussions}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

When using the multi-frequency vibration method with frequencies aligned to frequency bins and amplitude sums below 
the linearity threshold, the flutter derivatives $H_1^*$, $H_3^*$, $A_1^*$, and $A_3^*$ are accurately identifiable across all 
frequencies up to a superposition of three frequencies.
The derivatives $H_4^*$ and $A_4^*$ exhibit minimal errors, while $H_2^*$ 
and $A_2^*$ show the most significant deviations.
Despite maintaining the amplitude summation below the linearity threshold, 
considerable deviations appear in the flutter derivatives $A_2^*$ and $H_2^*$ across superposition schemes.
These deviations are due to phase changes identified in the amplitude study of Chapter 4. An important consideration is determining which result is representative.
Because, in real-world scenarios, externally applied motion is typically not purely sinusoidal;
therefore, the multi-frequency approach provides a comparably better representation of flutter derivatives $H_2^*$ and $A_2^*$ that captures variations due to phase.
Moreover, these derivatives have minimal influence on the critical wind speed.
However, as this conclusion is based on only four bridge 
deck configurations, it should not be generalized.
For other bridge deck properties, these derivatives may be critical.
In such cases, the choice between single- and multi-frequency methods should be carefully investigated.
% Future studies 
% investigating which bridge deck characteristics cause $A_2^*$ and $H_2^*$ to dominate the critical wind speed would be valuable, 
% as this deviation is not localized to this cross-section alone but rather inherent to pitch motion behavior in flutter analysis.
From multi-frequency simulation Campaign II, if the sum of amplitudes exceeds the linearity threshold, the flutter derivatives 
become highly inaccurate.
Importantly, this nonlinearity is detectable in spectral analysis. Therefore, researchers or engineers using 
multi-frequency simulations are advised to design frequency combinations that align with frequency bins, enabling FFT analysis 
to verify the absence of nonlinearity.
Accurate flutter derivative results are limited to superpositions of up to three prescribed motions.
At the fourth prescribed motion, considerable inaccuracy appears across all derivatives and significantly affects the identified critical velocity.
This is due to the high ratio of aerodynamic forces between frequency components within a superposition.
These force ratios become high at lower reduced velocities. Notably, this limitation is not detectable in spectral 
analysis and is localized to specific reduced velocities where force magnitudes are small.
It is therefore recommended to plot aerodynamic forces versus reduced velocity after multi-frequency simulations to verify that force ratios remain acceptably small.
These ratios can be reduced implicitly by selecting higher reduced velocities, where frequencies are lower and aerodynamic 
forces exhibit minimal variation.
This is advantageous for the multi-frequency method because the primary objective of the method is to reduce the simulation time.
Higher reduced velocities, corresponding to lower frequencies, require more time steps and simulation time.
For example, superposing the two highest reduced velocities can halve the simulation time compared to combining four lower reduced velocities.
Therefore, multi-frequency simulations with up to four superposed frequencies are recommended for the higher reduced velocity 
range, while single-frequency simulations are advisable for the lower reduced velocity range.
For heave versus pitch motion comparisons, the choice between single- and multi-frequency methods is subjective.
In summary, with respect to the reliability of multi-frequency methods for heave motion, superimposing the four highest reduced 
velocities yields the highest confidence in derivative accuracy.
For pitch motion with the four highest frequencies, results 
are more subjective, and single-frequency simulations are recommended for the four lowest frequencies in both heave and pitch 
motion cases.

\section{Guideline for Multi-Frequency Simulations}
% ============================================================

Based on the results obtained above, the following general guidelines are proposed for conducting multi-frequency simulations.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Determine the number of superpositions.} The recommended number is 3 or 4. Based on the 
          findings from this investigation, superpositions of up to three prescribed motions yield highly 
          accurate flutter derivatives across all frequencies.
          At the fourth prescribed motion, considerable 
          inaccuracy begins to appear in the lower reduced velocity range, 
          which can significantly affect the identified critical velocity.
          The limitation governing the maximum 
          number of superpositions is governed by the relative aerodynamic forces between frequency components 
          within a superposition and this shall be overcome by using different prescribed motion amplitudes to maintain constant aerodynamic force accross the frequencies.
          Also, superposing more than four frequencies does not yield a substantial reduction in 
          computational cost.
          This is because, when superposing higher frequencies (lower reduced velocities), 
          fewer time steps are saved (Figure \ref{fig:Overall Combinations - Total Time Saved vs No. of Superpositions}).

    \item \textbf{Target the lower frequency range.} Superpositions should preferably be used at the lowest 
          frequencies (i.e., those at the highest reduced velocities).
          This approach provides two advantages: first, it reduces error contributions that are prevalent at lower reduced velocities where 
          force amplitude ratios become large;
          second, it reduces overall simulation time. The 
          primary reason for high computational time is the lower frequencies (higher reduced velocities), 
          which require more time steps.
          Thus by superposing the lower reduced velocities, simulation time can be 
          reduced twofold compared to superposing four high frequencies (lower reduced velocities) 
          (Figure \ref{fig:Higher Ur vs Lower Ur - Total Time Saved vs No. of Superpositions}).

    \item \textbf{Select one independent frequency per superposition.} The independent frequency (lowest frequency) 
          is the reference for determining the frequency 
          bins and stable time period.
          This frequency should be chosen such that its frequency bin spacing 
          produces a definite number, ensuring that all dependent frequencies fall exactly on the frequency bins.

    \item \textbf{Determine dependent frequencies.} Derive the dependent frequencies within each superposition such that they fall exactly within 
          the frequency bin of the independent frequency.
          A detailed procedure for this calculation is shown at 
          the start of this chapter.
          When selecting frequencies for superposition schemes, it is essential 
          to avoid choosing two frequencies that are consecutive in the frequency bin list.
          If one frequency does not align with a bin due to incorrect selection, the result at the neighbouring frequency will be affected by spectral leakage.
          Even if a frequency of interest falls exactly within a 
          frequency bin, spectral leakage from a neighbouring frequency that does not fall within a bin can 
          affect the extracted amplitude and phase at that frequency.

    \item \textbf{Select amplitudes.} Amplitudes must be selected such that the total amplitude (sum across all frequencies in a superposition) 
          remains below, for heave 5\% of the bridge deck width and for pitch, an angle of 3°.

    \item \textbf{Post-processing and verification:} Apply the Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT) to the 
          aerodynamic forces and moments for the extraction of individual frequency components.
          The frequency spectrum must be analysed for signs of nonlinearity, which manifest as higher-order harmonic terms or as spectral leakage.
          A spectrum containing only the static 
          component, the forced-motion response components, and the vortex shedding component indicates that the 
          superposition guidelines has been correctly applied.
\end{enumerate}
