\chapter{Single Frequency Simulations}

This chapter investigates the linearity of flutter derivatives using single-frequency forced-motion simulations with 
prescribed heave and pitch motions. First a brief overview on CFD is presented. 
The computational setup and mesh convergence are then established to ensure the 
reliability of the simulations. The motion amplitudes are systematically increased from small values (0.68\% heave ratio and 1° pitch) to larger levels 
(8.20\% heave ratio and 8° pitch), extending beyond the linear range. For each amplitude, flutter derivatives 
are computed across a range of reduced velocities. The aerodynamic response is analyzed using flutter derivative evaluation, spectral decomposition of force signals, 
phase relationship assessment, and pressure distribution study. This investigation is focused on the verification of Scanlan's 
linearity assumption and the applicability of linear superposition, while also identifying which analysis methods 
are most efficient in capturing different forms of nonlinearity. Based on this, a clear baseline is established for selecting 
appropriate motion amplitudes in the subsequent multi-frequency simulations.

\section{Computational Fluid Dynamics}

Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) refers to the numerical modelling of fluid flow problems. Numerical modelling are 
approximate methods for partial differential equations using mathematical techniques. Consider, 
for instance, the differential equation governing the bending of a beam under the assumption of first-order kinematics. 
The solution i.e., the deflection profile, can be obtained either analytically through direct integration or approximately 
via numerical methods such as the Finite Element Method (FEM). Analytical solutions offer the advantage of being continuous 
and exact across the entire domain; for any point on the beam, the deflection can be determined precisely. In contrast, 
numerical solutions lack such accuracy and provide results only at discrete sets of points. 

The fundamental equations governing fluid flow are the Navier-Stokes equations. Due to their complex, non-linear nature, 
analytical solutions do not generally exist for real-world engineering applications (a mathematical challenge famously 
highlighted by the Clay Mathematics Institute's Millennium Prize), necessitating numerical approaches. This section provides a 
brief overview of these governing equations, the numerical techniques used to solve them, and a outline to turbulence 
modeling.

\subsection{Numerical Solution of the Navier-Stokes Equations} 

When formulating the equations of motion for a fluid system, Eulerian system is used. 
In the Eulerian system, the observer moves with and tracks individual fluid particles over 
time. In the Eulerian framework, a fixed frame of reference is used, where the observer measures 
the properties of the fluid as it passes through fixed spatial locations.
The Navier-Stokes equations represent Newton's second law of motion adapted for fluid continua within an Eulerian framework, 
constrained by the principle of mass conservation.  


\begin{equation}
    \rho \left( \frac{\partial \mathbf{u}}{\partial t} + (\mathbf{u} \cdot \nabla) \mathbf{u} \right) = -\nabla p + \mu \nabla^2 \mathbf{u} + \mathbf{f}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
    \nabla \cdot \mathbf{u} = 0
\end{equation}

Because these equations are non-linear PDEs functioning in both time and space, numerical methods are required to convert 
them into a system of algebraic equations through spatial and temporal discretization. The most widely used techniques include the the Finite 
Volume, Finite Difference, and Finite Element methods. As the solver used in this thesis is based on the Finite Element 
Method (FEM), this approach is primarily discussed. In FEM, the strong form of the governing differential equations is 
transformed into an integral weak form. By discretizing the fluid domain into finite elements, the continuous problem is 
converted into a system of linear equations that can be solved computationally (refer to [Insert Citation] for a detailed 
mathematical formulation).

\subsection{Turbulence Modelling} 

The most precise methodology for solving fluid flows is Direct Numerical Simulation (DNS), which resolves the Navier-Stokes 
equations across all spatial and temporal scales. The primary limitation of DNS is its extreme computational cost: 
resolving all turbulent scales down to the Kolmogorov length scale requires an exceedingly fine mesh. To overcome this, 
turbulence is modelled rather than resolved directly. This leads to methods such as Reynolds-Averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS) 
and Unsteady RANS (URANS) and Large Eddy Simulation (LES), which significantly reduce simulation times. An intermediate approach, Large Eddy Simulation (LES), 
resolves the larger energy containing eddies while modelling the smaller. LES thus incurs a computational 
cost between that of DNS and RANS. 

In RANS and URANS, the Navier-Stokes equations are time averaged, yielding the Reynolds-averaged Navier-Stokes equations. 
This decomposition separates the instantaneous velocity into mean and fluctuating components. The averaging procedure introduces 
additional terms—the Reynolds stresses—which represent the effects of turbulence on the mean flow. This constitutes a closure 
problem, as additional equations are required to relate these unknown stresses to the mean flow variables. Turbulence models 
to solve this closure problem.

Most RANS models rely on the Boussinesq hypothesis, which relates the Reynolds stresses to the mean velocity gradients via a 
turbulent "eddy viscosity." This parameter mathematically acts as an artificial increase in the fluid's viscosity to account 
for the momentum transfer and energy dissipation caused by turbulent mixing. Formulating closure models for the Reynolds stresses 
through the Boussinesq hypothesis to obtain eddy viscosity is the primary objective of turbulence models such as the $k-\epsilon$ and $k-\omega$ 
formulations.

The $k-\epsilon$ turbulence model have high robustness in free-stream conditions but lacks near-wall accuracy, 
whereas the $k-\omega$ formulation excels at resolving boundary layers but exhibits excessive sensitivity in the deep 
fluid domain. To overcome these limitations, Florian Menter introduced the $k-\omega$ Shear Stress Transport 
(SST) model, which employes mathematical blending functions to dynamically transition between the near-wall $k-\omega$ 
approach and the far-field $k-\epsilon$ formulation. The $k-\omega$ SST model is used for all CFD simulations presented in this thesis. 


\section{Simulation Setup and Methodology}

\subsection{CFD Model} 

The 2-D CFD model is created using GiD (Figure \ref{fig:GiD_Model_2}). Since forced motion oscillation tests are conducted, the structure, i.e., the bridge 
deck cross-section, is modelled as an opaque object in the fluid domain without any physical parameters 
such as stiffness and mass. Hence, it is a one-way coupling. The bridge deck cross-section is taken from Sarkic
\cite{sarkic2012}, shown in Figure~\ref{fig:Sarkic_Section}. It is then externally oscillated by the solver with a 
specific frequency. Arbitrary Lagrangian-Eulerian (ALE) framework is used for this.\\

The fluid domain is meshed with finer meshes near the structure and with coarser meshes farther away to have optimal 
meshing. Hence, to produce smoother mesh transitions, the fluid domain is divided into five subregions as shown in Figure \ref{fig:GiD_Model_2}.

\subsection{Solver Information} 

The GiD model is solved with an open-source multiphysics solver, KratosMultiphysics \cite{dadvand2010}. The boundary conditions applied to the fluid domain are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:Virtual_Wind_Tunnel_Dimensions}. The turbulence is modeled using an Unsteady Reynolds-Averaged Navier-Stokes (URANS) approach with the 
$k$--$\omega$ SST turbulence model \cite{menter}, a turbulence intensity of 0.1, and a turbulent mixing length of 0.0588~m.\\

The velocity is set to 1 m/s, and the corresponding Reynolds number for the flow is $2.43*10^4$. The time step is 
taken as 0.002 seconds (the corresponding CFL is shown in Table~\ref{tab:mesh_convergence}). The total simulation time is divided into three phases: ramp-up time, stabilization time, and stable 
time period. In the ramp-up phase, the flow velocity is steadily increased from 0 to 1 m/s to avoid numerical instabilities. In the stabilization phase, the flow is allowed to stabilize. The stable time period is the duration used for analysis. Ramp-up time and stabilization time are determined based on the vortex shedding frequency of the section. From EN 1991, the Strouhal number of the section is taken as 0.1.\\ [0.1cm]

Vortex shedding frequency = $ \frac{0.1 * 1}{0.366} $ = 0.273 Hz \\ [0.1cm]
Ramp-up time = $ 2 * \frac{1}{0.273} $ = 7.32 seconds \\ [0.1cm]
Stabilization time = $ 2 * \frac{1}{0.273} $ = 7.32 seconds \\ [0.1cm]

For flutter response, stable time period is considered as the time taken for 10 complete forced motion oscillations. As each reduced velocity 
has a different forced motion frequency, the stable phase of the simulations is different. A higher reduced velocity will 
have a lower oscillating frequency and hence requires a longer simulation time and vice versa. Hence, its calculation is 
shown after the information about the prescribed motion frequency is provided in the consecutive chapters. %Check: if any other reduced velocity is taken, the stable time period is mentioned in the respective chapter.

\section{Influence of Amplitude on Flutter Derivatives}

The base assumption of the Multi-Frequency method is that the principle of linear superposition holds. This assumption is derived from Scanlan's linearity assumption, which states that for small heave and pitch amplitudes, 
the applied forced motion oscillation and the obtained aerodynamic forces are equivalent with a phase 
shift. Although the above two assumptions might appear to point to the same meaning, they can differ in 
certain cases. From Scanlan's linearity assumption, the obtained forces can be linear with respect to 
the prescribed motion, but the phase shift can be non-constant. Therefore, it becomes essential to validate the principle of linear superposition and study the 
linearity limit to ensure the Multi-Frequency method is valid and to create a framework for this method.\\

This section focuses on validating the assumption and extending the knowledge of the nonlinearity characteristics 
of Sarkic's section. Although investigating the nonlinearity characteristics of a particular cross-section may make 
the developed framework specific to that cross-section, this is done so that any unexpected discrepancies 
in the results of the Multi-Frequency method can be reasonably explained. These inferences from the nonlinearity study are then generalised 
when framing the procedure for the Multi-Frequency method.\\

The study is conducted by increasing the heave and pitch motion amplitudes and then examining the obtained 
flutter derivatives, aerodynamic forces, and their phases. The following results are studied to understand the nonlinearity characteristics of the section:

\begin{flushleft}
\hspace{2cm}1. Comparison of Flutter Derivatives \\
\hspace{2cm}2. Spectral Analysis \\
\hspace{2cm}3. Aerodynamic Force and Moment Analysis \\
\hspace{2cm}4. Pressure Distribution Study \\
\hspace{2cm}5. Amplitude Impact on Critical Wind Speed \\
\end{flushleft}

\subsection{Comparison of Flutter Derivatives}

Figure~\ref{fig:Combined_Flutter_Derivatives} shows the flutter derivatives obtained for 6 heave 
and pitch amplitudes. It is observed that derivatives $H_1$, $H_4$, $A_1$, and $A_4$ are equal for amplitudes up to a 
heave ratio of 5.46$\%$. When it exceeds this value, the flutter derivatives vary. This validates the rule of thumb 
of a 5$\%$ linearity limit. For derivatives $H_2$, $H_3$, and $A_3$, the linear 
limit holds up to 3 degrees, although $H_2$ exhibits a slight nonlinear behavior within that range. This is slightly lower than the rule of thumb of 5 degrees. \\

From Figure~\ref{fig:Combined_Flutter_Derivatives}, derivative $A_2$ significantly deviates beyond 1.55 degrees. It should be noted that derivative $A_2$ even changes its sign at Amplitude Scheme 3 (pitch degree = 4.01). The reason for these behaviors is investigated and explained in the following sections.

\subsection{Spectral Analysis}

Spectral analysis is an effective tool to identify the presence of nonlinear terms in the obtained forces and 
validate Scanlan's linearity assumption. If the spectrum of the obtained forces contains higher-order sine terms 
other than the applied frequency, 
then nonlinear behavior is present. In this section, the spectrum of the obtained aerodynamic lift force and moment is 
analyzed for different amplitudes. A Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT) is used to obtain the spectrum of the forces. 
Plots of the spectrum corresponding to the reduced velocity of 21.86 are presented as representative samples, since this is the 
most affected by nonlinearity.\\

Figure~\ref{fig:Frequency_Domain_Analysis_Heave} shows the spectrum for amplitude scheme 6 for heave motion. The y-axis is presented on a log scale so that any noise 
is easily distinguishable. It is observed that the spectra of both the obtained lift force and moment are without spurious frequencies, 
indicating that the obtained forces are linear with respect to the prescribed motion. 
This is the same for all amplitude schemes, which is shown in Appendix~\ref{fig:Frequency_Domain_Analysis_Heave_scheme_0_to_3_U_21_86} and Appendix~\ref{fig:Frequency_Domain_Analysis_Heave_scheme_4_to_6_U_21_86}. \\

Figure~\ref{fig:Frequency_Domain_Analysis_Pitch} shows the lift force spectrum for amplitude schemes 3 and 4 for pitch motion (pitch degrees of 4.01 and 4.98). 
In general, the spectrum of pitch motion is noisy when visualized on a log scale. However on the normal scale, this spectrum is without spurious frequencies up to scheme 3. 
It is observed that the spectrum exhibits spurious frequency components from scheme 4, indicating the presence of higher-order sine terms and, 
therefore, nonlinearity starts to appear. For all degrees above this pitch degree, the spectrum contains spurious frequencies. This is  
presented in Appendix~\ref{fig:Frequency_Domain_Analysis_Pitch_scheme_0_to_3_U_21_86} and Appendix~\ref{fig:Frequency_Domain_Analysis_Pitch_scheme_4_to_6_U_21_86}. This case is the same for the pitch moment spectrum as well. Hence, it can be concluded that the linearity limit for pitch motion is between 4 and 5 degrees, 
which is slightly lower than the rule of thumb of 5 degrees.\\

On comparing Figure~\ref{fig:Combined_Flutter_Derivatives} and Figure~\ref{fig:Frequency_Domain_Analysis_Pitch}, 
It is observed that the nonlinearity in flutter derivatives $A_2$ and $H_2$ starts to occur at a lower pitch degree (3.09 degrees) 
than the linearity limit which was obtained from the spectrum analysis (4.98 degrees). 
Therefore, the deviation in flutter derivatives A2 and H2 for the scheme 3 and 4 does not arise from nonlinearity in the forces themselves. Although spectral analysis approximately validates the 
rule of thumb of 5 degrees for pitch motion, 
the flutter derivatives exhibit nonlinearity at a smaller pitch angle. This is investigated in the next section with a study on aerodynamic forces and phases.\\

\subsection{Aerodynamic Force and Moment Analysis}

In this section, the obtained aerodynamic forces and moments are compared against the prescribed motion 
amplitude to understand the nonlinearity characteristics of forces and moments. This is also 
conducted for the phase of aerodynamic forces and moments.\\

Figure~\ref{fig:Displacement_Vs_Force} shows plots of prescribed motion amplitude vs aerodynamic force and moment, and 
prescribed motion amplitude vs phase of aerodynamic force and moment, for both heave and pitch motion. These are plotted for 
a reduced velocity of 21.86. It is observed that for heave motion, the lift force and moment amplitude 
exhibit linear behavior with respect to the prescribed displacement. Furthermore, the phase of the force and 
moment is constant. For pitch motion, the lift force and moment show linear behavior up to a pitch degree of 5
(a mild nonlinearity is present, and this is the reason minor noise components are obtained in the spectrum even in the lower amplitude scheme for pitch in the previous section). This is again consistent with the rule of thumb of 5 degrees and 
spectral analysis and therefore validates Scanlan's linearity assumption.\\

However, the phase is not constant either for the lift force or moment after 3 degrees. To facilitate the comparative 
study of the phase alongside flutter derivatives, their relationship is briefly reiterated. Derivatives $H_1$, $A_1$, $H_4$, and $A_4$ are associated with the heave motion aerodynamic 
lift, moment, and their phase. Derivatives $H_2$, $A_2$, $H_3$, and $A_3$ are associated with the pitch motion aerodynamic 
lift, moment, and their phase. $H_2$ and $A_2$ are associated with the phase of the pitch motion aerodynamic 
lift and moment.\\ 

On comparing Figure~\ref{fig:Displacement_Vs_Force} and Figure~\ref{fig:Combined_Flutter_Derivatives}, 
it is clear that the nonlinearity in flutter derivatives $A_2$ and $H_2$ is due to the phase change. 
On examining Figure~\ref{fig:Displacement_Vs_Force}-{h}, the phase of the moment (related to derivative $A_2$) changes 
from positive to negative at 3 degrees (scheme 3). Further, it can be observed in Figure~\ref{fig:Combined_Flutter_Derivatives} 
that at 3 degrees, derivative $A_2$ changes its sign. This confirms the sign change in $A_2$ is caused by the phase change. It is therefore concluded 
that the nonlinearity in derivatives $A_2$ and $H_2$ is not due to nonlinearity in the obtained forces, 
but due to the phase change. This explains why the force spectrum remains without spurious frequencies even when nonlinearity is present. A similar behavior was observed by \cite{noda2003} and \cite{lin2019}.
(A detailed study on reasoning this behavior and comparative analysis with the 
referenced paper is provided in the next subsection on pressure distribution analysis).\\ 

This observation points to the earlier discussion that Scanlan's linearity assumption is not equivalent to the principle of linear superposition. 
Although the obtained forces may be linear with respect to the prescribed motion, linear superposition of the signals is not applicable at these amplitudes.\\  

Since the Figure \ref{fig:Displacement_Vs_Force}, plots linearity assessment only for the reduced velocity of 21.86, it is necessary to analyze the results for other reduced 
velocities before finalizing the conclusion. The results for all reduced velocities are compiled in Figure~\ref{fig:Compiled_Displacement_Vs_Force}. The x-axis is the same as before, the amplitude scheme, but the y-axis is modified.\\

This y-axis represents aerodynamic force divided by the prescribed motion amplitude and normalized with respect to the first amplitude scheme. The normalization 
is done because different reduced velocities will obtain different values of aerodynamic force per unit motion amplitude. With respect 
to phase, instead of normalizing, the relative error to the first amplitude scheme is taken as the y-axis. These allow for a 
better comparison of the nonlinearity characteristics across different reduced velocities. For better understanding, 
if the aerodynamic forces are linear, the plot should be a constant horizontal line at value of 1. If the phase is constant, 
the plot should similarly be a horizontal line at a value of 0 (indicating error is zero). Note that the $y$-axis limits are not uniform across subplots (this should be considered 
when comparing the degree of nonlinearity between the two figures). The non-uniform limits are 
kept to capture the variations in nonlinearity even when the overall error is small.\\
    
It is observed that for heave motion, the moment and its phase (\ref{fig:Compiled_Heave_Moment_vs_Amplitude} and \ref{fig:Compiled_Heave_PhaseMx_vs_Amplitude}) remain constant up to amplitude scheme 5 (heave ratio of 5.46) 
for reduced velocities above 1.71. This is equivalent to the behavior observed earlier for the reduced velocity of 21.86 
(Figure~\ref{fig:Displacement_Vs_Force}). The reduced velocities of 1.71 (frequency = 1.6 Hz) and 0.85 (frequency = 3.2 Hz) alone show significant deviation 
because they are affected by the vortex shedding effect. Spectral leakage from vortex shedding makes capturing derivatives at higher frequencies (1.6 and 3.2 Hz) difficult. Similar behavior is observed for the heave lift force as well, which presented in Appendix~\ref{fig:Appendix_Complete_Compiled_Displacement_Vs_Force}.\\

For pitch motion, the moment exhibits slight nonlinearity, as seen before for the reduced velocity of 21.86 (Figure~\ref{fig:Displacement_Vs_Force}). 
Another observation is that the influence of nonlinearity in phase increases with increasing reduced velocity (decreasing frequency). This further validates 
the behavior observed in Figure~\ref{fig:Combined_Flutter_Derivatives}, 
where the nonlinearity in $A_2$ and $H_2$ is less at lower reduced velocities.\\ 

To conclude, although Scanlan's assumption of linearity holds for the 
obtained forces, aerodynamic derivatives may still exhibit nonlinearity. Moreover, this 
nonlinear behaviour cannot be detected via spectral analysis.

\subsection{Pressure Distribution Study}

As mentioned earlier, a similar nonlinear behaviour of derivatives $A_2$ and $H_2$ was observed by \cite{noda2003} and \cite{lin2019}. 
However, in those works, the linearity limits observed at a larger pitch 
angles than observed in this study. This difference raises the question of whether the same underlying physical mechanism 
is responsible for the phenomenon. Hence, in this subsection, the reason behind this behavior is analyzed and verified to determine whether it is 
similar to what is observed in the references.\\

In that paper, it has been explained that the phase shift is primarily due to changes in the flow reattachment 
point, which is the resulted in the variation in pressure distribution along the section. At small pitch amplitudes, 
the pressure field remains relatively consistent throughout the oscillation cycle. The flow around the structure does not undergo 
substantial changes between the positive and negative peaks of the pitch motion amplitude. At larger pitch amplitudes, the pressure 
distribution varies highly over the cycle because the flow field exhibits significant alterations during 
the peak pitch motion. This is the cause of the observed phase deviation.\\

To perform a similar study, pressure distributions for the present simulation were extracted and compared across different pitch amplitudes. Time instants were chosen 
to cover one full oscillation cycle, starting from pitch degree 0 (at a simulation time of 32 seconds) to the maximum negative peak rotation at 38 seconds 
(as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:Prescribed Displacement}).\\

% Figure~\ref{fig: Comparative Pressure Distribution Amplitude Scheme - 0 Vs 6 - Pitch Motion} shows the pressure distribution 
% around the cross-section at several time steps for the amplitude scheme 0 and 6. The pressure field 
% remains relatively constant across the different phases of the motion. In contrast, for amplitude scheme 6
% the pressure distribution varies highly.\\ 

Figure~\ref{fig: Comparative Pressure Distribution Amplitude Scheme - 0 Vs 6 - Pitch Motion} shows the pressure distribution plotted around the 
bridge cross-section for amplitude schemes 0 and 6 (pressure inward represents negative pressure). It is observed that for scheme 0, the pressure distribution
is consistent among all 4 time steps. However for the amplitude scheme 6, it varies highly. 

Although the distribution of pressure itself changes, this behavior mainly affects the pitching moment (and therefore derivatives $A_2$ and $H_2$) 
rather than the lift force (related to $A_3$ and $H_3$). This is because the integrated lift force remains approximately constant 
even at higher pitch angles, but the distribution of pressure on the structure's surface changes considerably. As a result, the effective center 
of pressure (the moment arm weighted by the pressure distribution) shifts during the cycle, which is directly 
reflected in the pitching moment. Since the lift force is the net vertical integration, it is considerably less 
sensitive to these redistributions. This observation is consistent with the observations from the references, and 
therefore it is concluded that a similar mechanism is responsible 
for this discrepancy in the derivatives $A_2$ and $H_2$.\\

Finally, it should be noted that the onset of this nonlinear behavior in pitch motion is strongly 
influenced by the breadth-to-depth ratio of the section. Hence, the amplitude threshold 
at which phase nonlinearity becomes significant will vary across different cross-sectional shapes.

\subsection{Amplitude Impact on Critical Wind Speed}

Finally, the influence of this nonlinearity on the critical velocity is studied. For the calculation of critical velocity, structural parameters are required. Structural parameters of four existing bridges are taken from - [\cite{diana2020}, \cite{xin2007}, \cite{zhan2012_Tacoma_Narrows}]. Their structural properties are summarized in Table~\ref{tab:bridge_configs}.\\

The sensitivity of the critical wind speed
to errors in flutter derivatives depends on two factors. First, whether the reduced velocity
range at which errors occur coincides with the critical flutter range of the bridge. Second,
the structural properties of the bridge determine which flutter derivative plays a dominant
role in the critical velocity prediction. To address the latter factor, the structural 
parameters of four bridges are analyzed.\\ 

Figure~\ref{fig:CriticalVelocity_Vs_Amplitude_Scheme} shows the critical velocity obtained for all amplitude schemes. 
It is observed for all three bridge configurations (except Humen) that up to amplitude scheme 5 there is less influence on 
the obtained critical velocity. For Humen, after amplitude scheme 3, the critical velocity varies significantly. This indicates that this
bridge configuration might be more sensitive to the $A_2$ and $H_2$ derivatives. The reason that no major deviation is observed 
in the critical velocity (up to scheme 5) is that all the critical reduced velocities are below 12 (Figure~\ref{fig:ReducedVelocity_Vs_Amplitude_Scheme}). 
As the errors in flutter derivatives are significant in the higher reduced velocities, 
the critical velocity did not show significant variation.

\section{Observations and Discussion}

The linearity limit for heave motion agrees with the rule of thumb; therefore, the 
Multi-Frequency method should remain valid as long as the amplitude remains within this limit. Scanlan’s assumptions and the principle of superposition 
are not equivalent and may differ; this discrepancy is observed for pitch motion. Therefore, when 
validating the Multi-Frequency method, it is not necessary to obtain accurate derivatives for $A_2$ and $H_2$. Although 
the nonlinearity in $A_2$ and $H_2$ has been established in multiple research papers, the finding from this investigation is that 
the present model exhibits nonlinearity at a considerably lower pitch degree for Sarkic's cross-section.
